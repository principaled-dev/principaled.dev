# Introduction

Organizational change is inevitable. Departments merge, reporting lines shift, and new leaders step in. Sometimes that means a set of established, senior directors taking over a department that previously served as their customer. They bring credibility, a track record, and experience. They also bring a mandate for change.

That mandate can be a powerful thing. Fresh eyes see problems that familiarity has made invisible. But there's a failure mode that shows up with remarkable consistency: the new leadership starts dismantling before understanding why things were built the way they were.

Not just technology. Organizational structures, roles and responsibilities, ownership boundaries, agreements between teams. These things weren't arranged arbitrarily. They were carved out through hard-won understanding, negotiation, and trade-offs. Tear them down without understanding them, and you risk breaking the connective tissue that holds everything together.

There's a principle for this. It's called Chesterton's Fence.

# What Is Chesterton's Fence?

> There exists in such a case a certain institution or law; let us say, for the sake of simplicity, a fence or gate erected across a road. The more modern type of reformer goes gaily up to it and says, “I don’t see the use of this; let us clear it away.” To which the more intelligent type of reformer will do well to answer: “If you don’t see the use of it, I certainly won’t let you clear it away. Go away and think. Then, when you can come back and tell me that you do see the use of it, I may allow you to destroy it.”  
> — <cite>G.K. Chesterton [^1]</cite>

[^1]: https://fs.blog/chestertons-fence/

Put simply: if you come across a fence in the middle of a road and don't know why it's there, don't remove it until you find out. Someone put it there for a reason. You might disagree with the reason. You might find it outdated. But you need to know the reason before you can make that call.

In engineering and leadership terms: existing decisions, systems, and processes weren't made in a vacuum. They were responses to constraints, trade-offs, incidents, and realities that may not be immediately visible to someone arriving fresh. That architecture you think is overcomplicated? It might exist because a simpler version caused a production outage, or because the team got burnt by a similar approach elsewhere in the architecture. That awkward ownership boundary between teams? It might be the result of a painful negotiation that finally made collaboration possible.

This doesn't mean every fence is sacred. It means understanding is a prerequisite for change. Not permission. Not consensus. Understanding. Know *why* the fence exists, and then decide whether to keep it, move it, or tear it down.

# The New Broom Problem

There's an old saying: "A new broom sweeps clean." New leadership feels pressure, both internal and external, to make their mark, prove their value, and differentiate from what came before. The instinct to reshape things in your own image is human. But when that instinct isn't tempered by curiosity, the result is predictable.

The failure mode looks like this: existing decisions are treated as obstacles rather than artifacts of reasoning. What's already been built gets dismissed without anyone asking why it was built that way. The implicit message to the team is unmistakable: *what you've done so far doesn't matter.*

The people who built those systems, who made those decisions, who navigated those constraints? They're still there. They had context. They made trade-offs under conditions the new leader hasn't encountered yet. Ignoring that context doesn't just risk technical harm. It erodes trust. It signals that experience within the team isn't valued. And once that signal is sent, it's very hard to unsend.

# Respect for the Existing System

There's a particular flavour of this that deserves its own attention. It's the attitude that anything built before the new leader arrived is inherently suspect or flawed — not because it was evaluated, but because it wasn't *theirs*. They didn't have a hand in developing it, and so they don't trust it — because it's not yet second nature to them.

That statement, whether spoken aloud or communicated through actions, carries a very specific message: *if it's not my strategy, it's not valid.* The work that architects and engineers poured into technical strategy, the guiding technical principles the team aligned on, the ownership they held over direction and design, the decisions they defended and refined over time. All of it, dismissed. Not because it was wrong. Not because it was evaluated and found lacking. But because it predates the new leadership.

This is disacknowledgement in its most damaging form. It doesn't just disrespect the work. It disrespects the people who did the work. It tells experienced professionals that their judgement was irrelevant, that their contributions were placeholders until the "real" leadership arrived.

The morale cost of sweeping statements like this is enormous. And the irony is that the institutional knowledge being dismissed is exactly the knowledge the new leader needs most. You can't build a sound strategy on top of a team that no longer trusts you to value their input.

The alternative is straightforward: be courteous with the existing leadership team. Work closely with them. They carry the context you lack, the relationships you haven't built, and the scars from problems you haven't encountered yet. They are not obstacles to be replaced with "your people." They *are* your people now. Treating them as such is how you gain the understanding that makes real change possible.

# First Principles != Starting From Zero

New leaders often justify the clean sweep by claiming they're "thinking from first principles." It sounds rigorous. It sounds principled. But there's a critical difference between first-principles thinking and starting from zero.

First-principles thinking asks: what are the underlying constraints? What problem was being solved? Are those constraints still valid? Have they changed? It starts with curiosity about what exists, asks the right people for input, and then reasons upward from fundamentals.

Starting from zero skips all of that. It assumes nothing of value preceded your arrival and re-derives everything from scratch. That's not rigour, but rather, arrogance wearing a methodology hat.

You can absolutely bring fresh perspective and challenge assumptions. But challenge is different from dismissal. "Why did we do it this way?" signals curiosity vs. "we're not doing it that way anymore" without asking why is scorched earth. The first approach builds understanding and earns trust whereas the second destroys both. Worse still is when the latter isn't even a conversation, but a directive. No discussion, no input, just a decision handed down and enforced.

And it's not just *whether* things get questioned. It's *how*. There's a world of difference between "Help me understand why this exists" and "I don't understand this, so unless someone speaks up right now, I'm getting rid of it." The first invites collaboration. The second is an ultimatum dressed as an open door. It puts the burden on the team to justify their own work under pressure, rather than on the leader to do the work of understanding.

There's an even more damaging variant: when the existing team actively extends the invitation to onboard. Architects share their strategy documents. Engineers offer their design docs. Operation Leads offer walkthroughs of how things operate. The homework is laid out, ready to be done. And it gets ignored. Questions get raised about decisions that are already documented, by someone who hasn't read the documentation. "Have you had a chance to review this?" "No, but I'm questioning it anyway."

Do the homework. This is non-negotiable. Not doing so isn't just inefficient. The optics are devastating. It tells the team that their work isn't worth reading, that their effort to bring you up to speed was wasted, and that your opinions will be formed regardless of the evidence they've already prepared. Few things erode credibility faster than questioning decisions you haven't bothered to understand.

# Curiosity Before Demolition

The principled approach is simple in concept, though it requires discipline in practice: lead with questions, not conclusions.

Before changing anything, ask:

- "What problem does this solve?"
- "What was the context when this decision was made?"
- "What constraints were you working under?"
- "What did you try before landing on this?"
- "What would you change if you could do it again?"

That last question is the most powerful. It doesn't just gather information. It invites the team into the change process rather than sidelining them. It honours their experience while opening the door to improvement. It says: *I trust that you've thought about this, and I want to build on what you know.*

But curiosity can't stop at the technical. It needs to extend to the people and the organizational structure around them. New leaders sometimes limit their engagement to direct reports, treating the org chart as a boundary for conversation rather than a reporting structure. But the people with the deepest context aren't always the ones on your direct line. If you don't take the time to learn who holds what role and why, you risk misreading their contributions entirely. Work that was central to the team's direction gets dismissed as overreach, simply because the new leader never learned it was their job.

When you apply that curiosity across the technical, the people, and the organizational level, the result speaks for itself. You might still tear down the fence. But now you know what's on the other side. You've built context. You've earned trust. And you have the team's buy-in to make the change, because they can see you've done the work to understand before you decided to act.

# When the Fence Should Come Down

This isn't an argument for preserving everything. Some fences are outdated. Some were built around constraints that no longer exist. Some were genuinely mistakes. Change is not only acceptable but necessary.

The key is that you earn the right to tear down a fence by first understanding it, and you earn that understanding through respect and genuine curiosity. That's what builds the trust that gives you the legitimate mandate to act, not the one handed down from above. Once you understand the reasoning, you can make a principled case for change. You can explain what's different now, why the old approach no longer serves the team, and what the new direction looks like. That's a case the team can rally behind, because they can see you've done the work.

Leaders who skip this step may get short-term velocity. They may even be right about some of the changes they make. But they accumulate long-term resentment and blind spots. The fences they tore down had reasons. Those reasons don't disappear just because the fence is gone. They resurface as problems the existing team saw coming but couldn't prevent. And it's those same people who end up fixing the fallout, carrying the quiet resentment of having been right but not heard. The words "I told you so" hang in the air, but never get said, because the power dynamic doesn't allow for that kind of honesty.

That silence is where trust and respect go to die. Most people extend both by default, carried by a track record and the goodwill that comes with a new start. But that initial trust and respect only carry you so far. They're a loan, not a gift. And once broken, they're almost impossible to rebuild.

# Conclusion

New leadership is an opportunity, not a mandate for demolition. Fresh perspective is genuinely valuable. But fresh perspective without curiosity is just ignorance with authority.

Chesterton's Fence is, at its core, a first-principles argument. You can't reason from fundamentals if you skip the step of understanding what those fundamentals are in the current context. And that context isn't just technical. It lives in the people, the organizational structure, and the hard-won principles the team aligned on. The fence is there for a reason. Find the reason first. And if you haven't found it yet, at the very least show others that you're actively seeking it.

The path less principled tears down fences without understanding why they exist, treats existing work and the people behind it as obstacles to be cleared, enforces change through authority rather than understanding, and mistakes disruption for progress.

The principled path leads with curiosity, respects the context and the people that came before, and earns the legitimate mandate to change things. Not the one handed down from above, but the one earned from the team.

If you're stepping into new leadership, let this be the lens you lead through. And if you're a peer watching it unfold, you might be the only one with the standing to say something. Speak up.
